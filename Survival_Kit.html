Survival Kit

First we shape our tools, thereafter our tools shape us.

Useful preliminary considerations

In the following sections, a "personal survival kit" is not considered to be an additional/separated pack, but rather a list comprising all the most essential survival gear one should carry on a wilderness expeditions.
The alternatives are usually two: buy a prefabricated kit (you can find plenty on the internet) or make your own. The second option will most likely require more time, investigation, and background knowledge, but it's also usually preferred by experts. The reasons are many:

  • Purchasing survival items separately will allow you to familiarize with their characteristics and to test their reliability independently from the rest of the gear. Unprepared amateurs typically purchase prefabricated kits and never really inspect them. They trust the fact that carrying around such gear will be enough in case of emergency. You don't want to do that. Learn how to use the tools you buy before going off-the-grid.
  • There's no 'one fits all' personal survival kit. The amount of weight you can bring with you is limited and varying circumstances (e.g. climatic conditions, type of regions and activities, ) will require different tools.
  • Most of preassembled survival kits on the market tend to exploit the unexperienced eye of the average buyer and make profits over cheap quality tools or useless additional gadgets.

Belt Knife

The first item in your personal survival kit should always be a belt knife. The knife represents a kit within your kit. It has an incredibly large variety of uses (splitting wood, twisting and prying objects, shape components for traps and shelters, etc.) Keep your knife sharp! You can carry around a sharpening stone too, or you might be able to find one. Any fairly smooth stone will work.

The essentials

What follows is a brief list of items that you should always include in your personal survival kit. They represent the pillars of your survival equipment. Try to find some smart ways to keep them well organized.

  • Belt knife and optional sharpening stone: We already talked about it. Bring also a replacement to be extra safe.
  • Bandana: Keeps your head safe from the sun and can be used as an emergency bandage or cool compress. The more colorful the bandana, the better. We want to be seen.
  • Lighter, matches and candles: You'll need to be able to start a fire. Now, the most reasonable thing to do is to pack both some lighters and matches, since both items have different pros and cons. For instance, matches will work also in freezing environments while lighters are more reliable in case of rain and can be used one-handedly. Experts suggest butane lighters, especially in case of challenging weathers. A candle will instead help you get the fire going. If you want to be a real pro, carry a magnesium flint striker to use a signaling device. In any case, try to find some small waterproof recipients – possibly made of metal - to keep all these tools as dry as possible. And remember: make sure you’ve practiced enough with these items before starting your expeditions.
  • Fire­starting tinder: You’ll be able to find many alternatives on the market (pastes, pellets, etc.). You can gather more tinder as you proceed with your off-grid journey, such as small pieces of bark or dried grass.
  • Duct tape: Duct tape can be useful for a multitude of necessities. One can use it while building a shelter, to repair just about any type of equipment, or to keep a fire alive (since it tends to burn slowly). Tape can obviously prove effective in fixing bandages to heal wounds. Let's point it out again: the more colorful, the better. Colored duct tapes exist.
  • A metal cup: Useful for drinking and boiling water!
  • Dried food: Having food at your disposal will improve your physical and psychological performance. There are plenty of options, look online for the one that fits your necessities. Remember, you are looking for fat and protein.

Carrying Case

The container you choose for carrying your kit must be metallic, capacious and at the same time easy to carry. It should be protective, sealable and waterproof. The ideal case should both allow you to bring and boil water.
